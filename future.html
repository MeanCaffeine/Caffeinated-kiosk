    • Solutions: A Better Future For Pedestrians

    Lower Speed Limits

    Studies by the Seattle Department of Transit have shown that lowering speed limits, even a little bit, can save lives.

    Speed Limits aren't being obeyed? Traffic Calming, Explained

    Even with enforcement, getting drivers to obey posted safe speeds can be difficult. Road design can help both slow down drivers and make it safer for all.

    Better Public Transit Means More Walkers and Fewer Cars

    Getting Pittsburghers within a couple blocks of their destination is key for taking cars off the road, and getting more feet on the ground. Expanded routes to all ends of the city and suburbs is important, but the key factor is frequency and reliabillity. Public transit routes needs to be reliable and frequent enough that riders shouldn't ever have to check a bus or train schedule.

    Making Car-Human interactions safer

    When you cross a street in Pittsburgh, you're most likely stepping into the street. Instead of people encroaching on car space, which is dangerous, why not make cars come into pedestrian space?

    This approach gives right-of-way to people, rather than machines. Besides the natural "speed bump" effect, it forces drivers to pay more attention. It puts the onus of safety onto the driver rather than the pedestrians who are at a much bigger risk.

    Protecting the Crosswalk: Universal No Turn On red

    So called "Turn On Red" laws allow drivers to bypass traffic signals and cross paths with pedestrians despite them having the right-of-way. This is incredibly dangerous, especially when combined with agressive drivers. Intersections with "Right On Red" rules result in aproximatley 23% more crashes involving pedestrians!
